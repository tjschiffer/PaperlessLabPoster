The Paperless Lab

Enabling Robust Process Development by Creating an Electronic Record of All Lab Data

Timothy J. Schiffer

Abstract

In the Cell Culture and Medium Development group of PMPD, small-scale experiments with large numbers of batches are run in quick succession and frequency. Large data sets are generated and used to be stored across a variety of paper records and unconnected lab computers. Tedious data mining and manual entry into structured databases resulted in an inability to fully compare and analyze experiments.

Many of these obstacles can be overcome by integrating new intuitive user interfaces with already existing systems. Replacing paper records and dry-erase boards with websites and computer programs capable of accepting real-time information directly from the lab leads to a reduction in information silos and eliminates double data entry. With a focus on automation and data-rich visualizations, the Paperless Lab initiative directly improves the analysis of cell culture batches and ultimately enhances our process understanding.

Immense Data Generation During Upstream Process Development

Data is generated and collected in support of manufacturing process development. The upstream process begins with a cryogenically frozen vial of cells which is thawed into a shaker flask. The culture is then successively diluted through a series of vessels as the cells remain in exponential growth, in which cells continuously divide. Once an appropriate amount of cells have accumulated, the culture is transferred to the production vessel, which ranges from a shaker flask for experiments with large condition sets to scale-up reactors for production of kilogram quantities for pre-clinical testing.

Throughout the process, offline and online data are generated and stored in the appropriate data collection system. In addition to process data, an enormous amount of information related to cell culture medium, manual tasks, and other user-generated data must be stored and viewed in effecient, highly-available data structures. By eliminating paper records and collecting lab generated data electronically, critical process decisions can be made with real-time data displayed in information-rich visualizations.

Enabling High Data Visibility With Valuable Data Storage Systems

While paper records and reusable writing surfaces have been used for in-lab collection of data since the dawn of science, recent technological advancements have allowed for data to be collected digitally and immediately stored in an appropriate network-connected storage system. By leveraging commercially available data-based applications and creating custom web-based tools and visualizations, information can be viewed and analyzed from any network connected device.

Intuitive Digital Batch Sheets Facilitate Daily Data Entry

Previous to the implementation of the Digital Batch Sheet application, lab personnel recorded information on paper batch sheets, later copying the relevant data into the LIMS system. This process was incredibly tedious, requiring values of be copied one at a time across separate web pages for each single value. Lack of data entry into LIMS resulted in an inability to compare batch data across experiments.

By replicating the functionally of the paper batch sheets in a user-friendly web application, 100% user compliance has been achieved.

Digital batch sheets allow for scanning sample information into the analyzer. Once the analyses results are parsed into LIMS, the results are displayed on the digital batch sheets. User generated data is entered directly into the application, bypassing the paper step by storing and retrieving the data in LIMS in addition to displaying real time results relevent to process decisions.

Ensuring Accurate Setpoints With A Visual Web Application

Previous to the Paperless Lab Initiative, critical setpoints would be written down or printed and manually entered into each control system for each individual bioreactor. By leveraging the bidirectional data-flow ability of the PI Historian System and a spreadsheet-like web application that allows for the entry of reactor setpoints, errors as a result of incorrect setpoint entry are eliminated.

Collaborative White Board For Task Coordination

Dry-erase white boards can be used for everything from task coordination to asset management. While white boards are an essential in-lab communication tool, they are limited in their ability to view historical data, be edited remotely, and are subject to poor handwriting. To overcome these shortcomings, the Microsoft OneNote application has been implemented to create a collaborative, highly-available workspace with a full audit trail.

Highly Visible Analyzer Results Increase Data Mining Efficiency

When all data is properly collected and stored in a highly-available database, data-rich visualizations can be created to display real-time equipment statuses and historical results. This leads to less searching for an available instrument as well as a phenomenal reduction in time spent data mining since all results are aggregated to a single source.

Summary

With implementation of web applications and automated data collection, data gaps and tedious tasks are eliminated. A high level of automation and implementation of intuitive applications will be essential as PMPD increases development throughput. The Paperless Lab initiative has saved thousands of work hours by reducing data mining while increasing the visibility and analyses of lab generated data by eliminating paper records for in-lab data collection.
